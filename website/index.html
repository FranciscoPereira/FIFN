How it works

Four steps, zero data exposure

Each insurer trains independently on its own servers. Only encrypted model weights travel over the network.

01
Pull global model
At the start of each round your node fetches the latest aggregated model from Flock.io's on-chain registry via IPFS.
02
Train locally
Fine-tune the model on your own claims data — on your own servers. Raw data never leaves your environment.
03
Submit encrypted weights
Your weight update is encrypted, uploaded to IPFS, and registered on-chain. No other party can decrypt it individually.
04
Get a better model
Flock.io's on-chain aggregator combines all updates into a stronger global model. Every node — big or small — benefits equally.

Benefits

Why small insurers win most

A large insurer already has enough data. FIFN levels the playing field.

Privacy by design

Raw claims data — policyholder names, addresses, medical records — never leaves your servers. Only encrypted model weight deltas are submitted, and only the aggregate is ever decrypted.

Enterprise-grade detection on a small-insurer budget

Members with as few as 800 labelled claims have seen AUC-ROC improvements of 4–6 percentage points — equivalent to years of in-house data collection.

Earn rewards for quality data

Flock.io's on-chain reward mechanism distributes FML tokens proportionally to how much your updates improve the global model. Good labels earn more.

Cross-insurer fraud rings detected

Staged accident rings, organised medical billing fraud, and serial policy fraud all span multiple insurers. A federated model catches what local models can't see.

Adapts to your fraud base rate

FedProx regularisation prevents the global model from being dominated by large nodes. Even a 0.5% fraud base rate is handled with per-node class-weight calibration.

Fully auditable on-chain

Every federation round — who submitted, when, what the resulting model CID is — is immutably recorded. Regulators and auditors get a complete, tamper-proof log.

FAQ

Common questions

Everything you need to know before joining.

Does FIFN ever see our claims data?
No. Raw claims data never leaves your environment. Your node trains locally on your own servers and submits only encrypted model weight updates. Flock.io's threshold secret-sharing means the aggregator sees only the combined result — never any individual node's weights.
What if my fraud labels are sparse or uncertain?
FIFN handles label heterogeneity. Each node calibrates its own class weights before training (tuned to your fraud base rate), and our FedProx regularisation prevents nodes with sparse or noisy labels from destabilising the global model. You can also pass a label-confidence score alongside your submission so the aggregator weights your update appropriately.
Can a competitor learn anything from our participation?
Cryptographically, no. Your weight delta is encrypted before it leaves your node, and the contract enforces a minimum of 5 participants per round before aggregation. Even if a competitor controls 4 of those other nodes, they cannot reconstruct your individual update from the aggregate. Differential privacy noise is added locally for additional protection.
What infrastructure do I need to run a node?
A Python 3.11+ environment with a GPU (optional but faster) and internet access. Our open-source client handles everything: model serialisation, encryption, IPFS upload, and on-chain submission. Most insurers run it on a single VM inside their existing cloud account. Typical round time is 35–50 minutes end-to-end.
How are FML rewards calculated?
Rewards are proportional to the improvement your update contributes to the global model's validation loss on a held-out benchmark set maintained by the Flock.io contract. Larger datasets and higher-quality labels tend to earn more. Updates that degrade the model earn nothing and may trigger a stake slash if the degradation is significant.
Is there regulatory precedent for this kind of data sharing?
Yes. Because no raw data is exchanged, FIFN is architecturally equivalent to a traditional model-sharing arrangement, not a data-sharing arrangement. Several EU and US jurisdictions have published guidance that federated learning of this type does not constitute transfer of personal data under GDPR or CCPA. We recommend legal review for your specific jurisdiction, and can provide a privacy impact assessment template.
